How is this calculated?
Understand the three components and how the score is derived
▼
Expense Health
Checks whether your monthly income is fully and realistically allocated. Around 100% is healthiest. Going above 100% means you are planning to spend more than you make.
Savings Rate
Looks at how much of your income goes to savings. Higher is generally stronger, but this is a signal, not a verdict.
Budgeting Coverage
Checks how well your monthly budget covers planned irregular costs like travel, furniture, and repairs.
Final score
The final number is the average of those three signals. Treat it as a planning shortcut, not as a precise judgement of your finances.

📅 Budgeting

Track your monthly budget — log spending and see what remains

💡
How does this work?
How your monthly budget from Expenses flows into the rolling balance here
▼
1. Your monthly budget tops it up each month
The category you connected to Budgeting in Expenses sets how much is added each month. That amount accumulates — anything unspent carries forward.
2. Log what you spend
Use the Spending Log to record irregular purchases — furniture, clothes, repairs, anything non-monthly.
3. Trips are included automatically
Trips planned in the Travel tab are automatically added to the Spending Log in the relevant months, so your travel budget is always reflected in the balance.
4. Read the rolling balance
The balance table shows what was available at the start of each month, what was spent, and what remains. A negative balance means you spent more than your monthly budget covered.

How much you save each month · how it compounds · what you retire with

How does this work?
Understand the calculation, what to fill in, and how to update it
▼
1. Set your retirement target
Choose your retirement age, expected real return, and target monthly income in retirement.
2. Add capital events
Use capital events for one-off amounts that boost your invested capital at a specific moment in time.
3. Add savings periods
Define how much you save per month, and add a new period whenever that amount changes.

The app combines capital events, monthly savings, and compound returns to estimate your retirement capital and projected monthly income.
Important: returns only apply if that capital is actually invested.

🧭 Retirement Setup

Capital is the total you have accumulated at any point in time — fed by three sources: capital events (one-off amounts like a bonus or inheritance), monthly savings (from your savings periods, added each month), and compound returns (your investments generating returns on top of what's already there). Monthly income (4% rule) i
—
Monthly income (4% rule)
How much you could withdraw every month without eating into your capital. Based on the 4% rule: take 4% of your total capital per year, divide by 12. It's a widely used benchmark for sustainable retirement income.
Years of compounding i
—
Years of compounding
The number of years between today and your retirement year. This is your most powerful lever — the longer your money is invested, the more returns build on top of previous returns. Even a few extra years makes a significant difference to the final number.
Capital lasts i
25+ years
Capital lasts
How long your capital would last if you started withdrawing at retirement using the 4% rule. If your investments continue to grow at 2–3% in real terms during retirement, the capital sustains itself indefinitely. This assumes you live off returns rather than drawing down the principal.
